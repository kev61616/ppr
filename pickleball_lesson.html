Introduction to PPR Level 2 Certification

Welcome to the PPR (Professional Pickleball Registry) Level 2 Certification interactive study guide. This comprehensive resource is designed to help you prepare for your Level 2 certification assessment.

As a Level 2 certified instructor, you'll possess the advanced skills, knowledge, and teaching abilities to effectively instruct and coach intermediate to advanced pickleball players. This certification represents a significant step in your professional development as a pickleball instructor.

Level 1 vs Level 2: What's Different?

Aspect Level 1 (Basic) Level 2 (Advanced)
Target Students Beginners, novices Intermediate, advanced players
Technical Knowledge Basic shots and rules Advanced techniques, detailed mechanics
Teaching Methodology Simple progression Hourglass format, detailed feedback
Strategy Focus Basic gameplay Advanced tactics, court positioning
Skill Demonstration Basic competency Advanced execution and analysis

Certification Requirements

To achieve PPR Level 2 certification, you must demonstrate proficiency in:

  1. Advanced teaching methods and lesson planning for intermediate/advanced players
  2. Detailed technical knowledge of all pickleball shots, including specialized techniques
  3. Ability to analyze and correct common technical errors
  4. Advanced strategic concepts for both singles and doubles play
  5. Professional conduct and safety protocols

Certification Tip

Practice your demonstration skills extensively. As a Level 2 instructor, your ability to clearly demonstrate proper technique is critical to the assessment process.

Advanced Grip Techniques

While Level 1 certification covers basic grips, Level 2 instructors must have a comprehensive understanding of all grip variations, their advantages, disadvantages, and appropriate applications in different play situations.

Continental Grip Mastery

The Continental grip is held like you're shaking hands with the paddle, with the V between thumb and index finger aligned with the edge of the paddle. It's the most versatile grip in pickleball.

Key Teaching Points:

  • V formation between thumb and index finger aligns with the edge of the paddle
  • Relaxed grip pressure - "hold the paddle like a bird"
  • Allows quick transitions between forehand and backhand without changing grip
  • Ideal for volleys, dinks, and net play

Common Teaching Error

Many instructors fail to emphasize the importance of a relaxed grip. A tight continental grip reduces wrist flexibility and touch, especially during dinking exchanges.

Eastern Forehand Grip

The Eastern forehand grip is similar to a tennis forehand grip, with the base knuckle of the index finger positioned on the third bevel of the paddle handle.

Applications:

  • Power groundstrokes from the baseline
  • Driving the ball with topspin
  • Third shot drives
  • Powerful put-away shots

Teaching Progression:

  1. Start with Continental grip as reference
  2. Rotate hand slightly clockwise (for right-handed players)
  3. Place index knuckle on third bevel
  4. Ensure comfortable paddle face alignment

V-Shape Grip

The V-Shape grip is a modification that focuses on creating a clear V-formation between the thumb and index finger, which aligns with the edge of the paddle.

Benefits:

  • Enhanced paddle face control
  • Improved feel for dinking and soft shots
  • Natural wrist positioning
  • Reduced risk of "paddle drag" on volleys

When to Teach:

Introduce the V-Shape grip to players who:

  • Struggle with consistent paddle face control
  • Need to improve soft-game precision
  • Have smaller hands or limited grip strength
  • Experience frequent mishits at the non-volley zone

Teaching Tip

When teaching the V-Shape grip, use the analogy of "shaking hands with the paddle" but emphasize that the V between thumb and index finger should point to the paddle edge, not the face.

Grip Selection Guide for Different Situations
Playing Situation Recommended Grip Rationale
Dinking at NVZ Continental or V-Shape Better control and touch for soft shots
Volleys Continental Quick transitions between forehand/backhand
Groundstrokes Eastern Forehand/Backhand More power and topspin potential
Serves Eastern or Continental Balance of power and control
Third Shot Drops Continental Better touch and control for soft shots
Overheads Continental or Eastern Power with stable paddle face

The Hourglass Lesson Format

The Hourglass Format is a structured teaching methodology that guides players through different game situations with varying levels of structure. It's named for its conceptual shape—starting wide with open play, narrowing for focused skill work, and then widening again to apply skills in game situations.

Format Structure:

  1. Open: Free play for observation
  2. Semi-Open: Guided play with specific focus
  3. Closed: Technical skill development
  4. Semi-Closed: Controlled practice drills
  5. Open: Application in game situations

Recommended Timeline:

Phase Time Allocation Activities
Open 10-15% Warm-up games, free play, observation
Semi-Open 15-20% Guided play, focused observation, discussions
Closed 30-40% Skill development, demonstrations, focused practice
Semi-Closed 15-20% Controlled drills, skill application exercises
Open 10-15% Games, competitive play, skill application

Implementation Tip

During the transition between phases, clearly communicate the shift in focus to students. This helps them mentally prepare for the change in structure and expectations.
